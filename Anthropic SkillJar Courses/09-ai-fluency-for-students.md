## Overview

The student-facing version of the AI Fluency Framework course. Teaches the 4D Framework (Delegation, Description, Discernment, Diligence) specifically for academic learning and early career contexts. Emphasises AI as a thinking partner that enhances learning rather than replaces effort — the "human in the loop" concept. Developed by the same Anthropic-Ringling-UCC partnership as the other AI Fluency courses.

**Target audience:** University students, recent graduates, early-career professionals learning to use AI responsibly in academic and career contexts  
**Prerequisites:** None  
**Cost:** Registration required  
**Format:** Section-based course with practical applications

## Full Curriculum

### Section 1 — Introduction and AI Fluency Framework
- The difference between **using AI** and being **AI fluent**
- Introduction to the 4D Framework for students:
  - Delegation: what to hand to AI vs. what to do yourself to actually learn
  - Description: communicating what you need clearly (prompt literacy)
  - Discernment: evaluating AI output with your own critical thinking
  - Diligence: ethical, responsible, and disclosed AI use in academic contexts
- The "thinking partner" framing: AI enhances your thinking, it doesn't replace it
- Why AI fluency (not just AI access) is what employers and educators actually value

### Section 2 — AI Fluency Framework Applications for Students

**Academic applications:**
- **Understanding concepts more deeply** — Using Claude as a tutor: ask it to explain concepts multiple ways, check your understanding, identify gaps. Key: you're the one learning, not Claude.
- **Research and synthesis** — Using AI to help survey literature, identify sources, organise findings. Key: critical evaluation of what AI surfaces vs. accepting it uncritically.
- **Writing and argument development** — Using AI to outline, get feedback on drafts, strengthen arguments. Key: your voice and argument must remain central; AI is the editor, you're the author.
- **Problem-solving and learning** — Working through problems with AI assistance while maintaining your own understanding. Key: can you solve the next problem without AI? If not, you didn't actually learn.

**Career and professional applications:**
- **Career planning** — Using AI to research industries, roles, and paths; drafting cover letters and CVs; interview preparation
- **Skill development** — Using AI to accelerate learning new skills (coding, analysis, writing)
- **Professional communication** — Using AI to draft emails, reports, and presentations while maintaining authentic professional voice

### Section 3 — Conclusion
- "Being the human in the loop" — maintaining agency, judgment, and responsibility
- AI fluency as a durable career skill: the skill is human-AI collaboration, not any specific tool
- Next steps in developing AI fluency beyond the course

## The Core Student Dilemma: Learning vs. Shortcutting

This course directly addresses the tension students face: AI can complete many academic tasks, but completing tasks isn't the same as learning. The 4D Framework helps students navigate this:

- **Delegation**: Should you use AI for this specific task? (High-stakes exam: probably not. Brainstorming topic ideas: yes.)
- **Description**: Did you provide enough context about what you're learning and why? (Better description → more educational AI interaction)
- **Discernment**: Can you evaluate whether the AI's output is correct? (If you can't evaluate it, you haven't learned enough yet)
- **Diligence**: Is your use of AI disclosed as required by your institution? Is your work genuinely yours?

## How to Operationalise in Our Workflow

*(Reframed for professional/team learning contexts)*

### For onboarding new team members to AI tools
The student framework maps to onboarding:

1. **Early weeks**: Use AI for exploration and learning (Delegation limited; Discernment practiced)
2. **Building competence**: Use AI to accelerate, not replace, skill development
3. **Full fluency**: AI as standard tool; all 4D habits internalised

### The "thinking partner" principle for professionals
Even experienced professionals should apply the student principle: if you can't evaluate the AI's output, you're not ready to use AI for that task in a professional context. This is the basis of a maturity model for AI use:

| Level | AI use pattern |
|---|---|
| Beginner | Using AI outputs without evaluation |
| Developing | Using AI outputs with basic fact-checking |
| Proficient | Critical evaluation with domain expertise |
| Expert | Strategic delegation; AI as reliable specialist tool |

### For team AI fluency programmes
- This course's framing is directly applicable to building a learning culture: the goal is fluency, not just tool access
- The career framing (AI fluency as durable skill) is persuasive for team members sceptical of AI adoption
- The "human in the loop" principle is a key cultural value to install early

### Note on audience
While designed for students, the concepts are directly applicable to:
- New hires being onboarded to AI tools
- Team members who have AI tool access but haven't developed judgment
- Anyone at the "I know Claude exists" stage but not yet at the "I use it fluently" stage
