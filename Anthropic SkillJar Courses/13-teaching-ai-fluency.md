## Overview

A course specifically for people who will teach the AI Fluency Framework to others in instructor-led settings. Distinct from "AI Fluency for Educators" (which is about using AI in your own teaching practice) — this course is about teaching the AI Fluency Framework itself as a curriculum. Created by Rick Dakan and Joseph Feller in partnership with Anthropic; supported by Ireland's Higher Education Authority.

The key question this course answers: "How can I teach and assess AI Fluency in instructor-led settings?"

**Target audience:** Faculty, instructional designers, corporate trainers, L&D professionals who want to deliver AI Fluency training to others  
**Prerequisites:** "AI Fluency: Framework & Foundations" (strongly implied); ideally also "AI Fluency for Educators"

## Full Curriculum

### Section 1 — Teaching Approaches
The pedagogical section on how to teach the 4D Framework effectively:

- **Teaching the 4D Framework** — How to introduce Delegation, Description, Discernment, and Diligence in a way that sticks; sequencing the framework; common misunderstandings students have
- **Active learning strategies** — The framework is best learned by doing, not listening; hands-on exercises, simulations, and real-task practice
- **Adapting to different disciplines** — How the 4D Framework applies differently in engineering vs. creative fields vs. business; customising examples for your audience's domain
- **Handling scepticism** — Common objections ("AI will take my job," "AI is just a fad," "this is just autocomplete"); evidence-based responses
- **Live demonstration techniques** — Showing the 4D Framework in action during teaching; using AI as a teaching tool within the class itself

### Section 2 — Assessment & Assignment
How to evaluate AI Fluency — the hardest part of teaching it:

- **Authentic assessment design** — Traditional tests can't assess AI fluency (AI can complete them). Design needed:
  - Process-based assessments: show your working with AI
  - Contextual assessments: real-world tasks with AI in the mix
  - Oral/discussion assessments: explain your AI use and decisions
- **Assignment design principles** — Assignments that require AI fluency, not just AI access:
  - Tasks where the student must critically evaluate AI outputs
  - Tasks where the student must make strategic delegation decisions
  - Tasks requiring disclosure and justification of AI use
- **Rubric development** — How to create fair, specific rubrics for the four Ds:
  - Delegation rubric: was the task appropriate to delegate? Was the scope appropriate?
  - Description rubric: was the prompt clear, contextual, and iteratively refined?
  - Discernment rubric: was the output critically evaluated with domain expertise?
  - Diligence rubric: was AI use disclosed, ethical, and appropriately scoped?
- **Portfolio assessment** — Using a portfolio of AI interactions to assess fluency development over time

### Section 3 — AI's Impact and Your Discipline
The discipline-specific and future-focused section:

- **How AI affects different disciplines** — Domain-specific analysis of what changes (and what doesn't) with AI
- **Curriculum implications** — What should be added to, modified in, or removed from a discipline's curriculum as AI becomes standard?
- **Future-proofing AI Fluency** — The 4D Framework is designed to be tool-agnostic; preparing students for AI systems that don't exist yet
- **Institutional considerations** — Academic integrity policies; disclosure requirements; equity issues with AI access

|---|---|---|
| Process-based assessment | Assess the AI collaboration, not just the output | Only way to truly evaluate AI fluency |
| 4D rubrics | Specific criteria for each of the four Ds | Makes grading fair and transparent |

## The Assessment Challenge in Detail

The central problem: if AI can complete an assignment, the assignment doesn't assess learning. Solutions from this course:

### Assessment types that work
1. **Dialogue-based assignments** — Submit your full conversation with Claude plus reflection: what did you ask? How did you evaluate the output? What did you change?
2. **Error-detection tasks** — Give students an AI-generated output with embedded errors; can they find them? (Tests Discernment)
3. **Delegation decisions** — Given a task, should you use AI for it? For which parts? Justify your decision. (Tests Delegation)
4. **Iterative prompting** — Show the evolution of your prompt across 5 iterations; what changed and why? (Tests Description)
5. **Ethical scenario analysis** — Given a situation, is this AI use appropriate? What would you disclose? (Tests Diligence)
6. **Oral examination** — Explain your AI use and reasoning face-to-face; AI can't be present. (Tests all four Ds authentically)

## How to Operationalise in Our Workflow

### Designing an internal AI Fluency training programme
This course gives everything needed to build an internal L&D programme around AI Fluency:

#### Programme design
1. **Module 1**: AI Fluency Framework (the 4D Framework) — 90 min
2. **Module 2**: Practical application — hands-on exercises with real work tasks — 2 hours
3. **Module 3**: Assessment of learning — participants demonstrate each D with their own work — 1 hour
4. **Module 4**: Discipline/role adaptation — how does this apply specifically to your work? — 1 hour

#### Assessment for professional teams
For corporate contexts, adapt the academic assessments:
- **Delegation portfolio**: 5 recent AI-assisted work tasks; for each: was it appropriate? What was your role vs. AI's?
- **Prompt evolution**: Pick a complex task you used Claude for; show your prompt iteration and what you learned
- **Discernment audit**: Take 3 AI outputs you've used at work; apply domain expertise — are they actually correct?
- **Diligence self-assessment**: Complete the 4D checklist for a recent project; what would you do differently?

#### Facilitation tips from the course
- Open with a live demo where you do a real task with Claude, narrating the 4D Framework in action — better than slides
- Let participants try things themselves before explaining — failure is a great teacher for Discernment
- Have participants bring their own work; don't use artificial examples
- Expect resistance from experienced domain experts who feel AI undermines their expertise — address Discernment first

### For our specific context
- This course is the path from "we use Claude" to "we teach others to use Claude well"
- The assessment design section is directly applicable to evaluating team AI fluency
- The discipline adaptation section is important: different roles in our team need different examples and framings
