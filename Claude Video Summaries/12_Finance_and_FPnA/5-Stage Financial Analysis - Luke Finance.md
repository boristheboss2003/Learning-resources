**Premise**

Claude is capable of institutional-grade analysis but only with right structure. Most people prompt it like a chatbot --- that\'s why outputs feel generic. Difference between fragile and board-ready analysis isn\'t intelligence --- it\'s sequence.

Five different financial analyses across the SAME use case. Each stage builds on the previous one.

**The Use Case --- Real Stakes**

Safety defect in high-volume equipment line forces recall of products already in field. Cash going out the door. Warranty claims stacking. Refunds. Legal exposure. Insurance disputes. Supplier negotiations. All at once.

**Real Data Available**

-   3 years of historical P&L.

-   Monthly shipment volumes.

-   Recall quantities.

-   Cash position.

-   Revolver capacity.

-   Recall cost assumptions.

-   Insurance limits and deductibles.

-   Supplier recovery estimates.

-   Covenant thresholds.

**Three Reasons Situation Is Serious**

1.  Cash flow drops almost instantly.

2.  No one knows true size of liability or how much is recoverable.

3.  Covenant pressure and reputational damage could affect future demand.

**Question CEO and Finance Team Need**

> *Can we absorb this or do we need to take capital action?*

**Stage 1 --- Descriptive (What Happened?)**

Establish liquidity and covenant position immediately after financial shock. If you don\'t get clarity on cash and covenant first, every decision after is built on unstated assumptions.

**Output: Executive Recall Snapshot Dashboard**

-   3-year P&L trend.

-   Units shipped vs units recalled comparison.

-   Current cash and revolver availability.

-   Projected 3-month cash impact.

-   Interest coverage ratio flagged against 3x covenant threshold.

-   Anything in breach clearly marked red/yellow.

**Verify Before Trusting**

-   126.3% recall rate --- confirm cumulative or monthly.

-   Starting cash and revolver availability validated with treasury.

-   Interest expense and 3x covenant threshold consistent with loan agreement.

-   Post-recall EBITDA correct.

-   Internal liquidity policy considered.

**Stage 2 --- Diagnostic (Why Did It Happen?)**

Break gross recall costs into structural vs recoverable exposure BEFORE any capital decision. Single recall estimate without driver breakdown overstates recoverability.

**Output: Recall Cost Driver Bridge**

-   Gross recall cost by driver: replacement, logistics, refunds, legal.

-   Recovery section: insurance recovery \$70.7M, supplier recovery \$8.3M, total \$78.9M (64.3% of gross).

-   Net exposure: \$43.9M (36.6% of liquidity).

-   Coverage ratio: 2.73x.

**Stakeholder Sign-Offs Required**

-   Operations confirms replacement cost per unit.

-   Legal reviews and approves legal reserve percentage.

-   Claims team signs off on insurance recovery percentage and timing.

-   Supplier fault and indemnity basis is contractually defensible.

-   Treasury confirms liquidity baseline.

**Stage 3 --- Predictive (What Will Happen?)**

Build 24-month cash forecast to identify exactly when liquidity trough hits. In recall situation, that trough is where companies actually get into trouble. Failures happen at month 2-3, not month 24.

**Output: cash_forecast_24M Sheet**

-   All 24 months populated.

-   Recall payouts spread: 40% month 1, 35% month 2, 25% month 3.

-   Insurance recovery sequenced months 3-8 (NOT immediate).

-   Supplier recovery: month 12.

-   Operating cash flow adjustments for churn/disruption.

-   Monthly ending cash.

-   Trough month identified: month 3, minimum liquidity \$17.7M, lowest cash \$38.5M.

-   Peak revolver draw: \$62.3M (77.8% of capacity).

-   Covenant breach in months 1-3 flagged. Revolver capacity not breached. Ending cash \$99M at month 24.

**Stage 4 --- Prescriptive (What Should We Do?)**

Compare base, downside, upside scenarios to determine capital strategy. Presenting only base case underestimates downside funding needs and delays equity discussions until liquidity is already tight.

**Output: Three-Column Scenario Decision Matrix**

  --------------------------------------------- -------------------------------------------------------------- -----------------------------------------------------------
  **Scenario**                                  **Funding Need**                                               **Recommended Capital Action**
  Base                                          None --- revolver draw \$50.9M                                 Revolver + waiver
  Downside (recall +30%, insurance denial 0%)   \$33.4M gap; revolver draw \$108.4M (exceeds \$80M capacity)   Immediate equity raise of \$35-50M, urgency under 30 days
  Upside                                        Self-funded                                                    Minimal action
  --------------------------------------------- -------------------------------------------------------------- -----------------------------------------------------------

**Verify**

-   Downside assumptions realistic --- insurer denial plausible?

-   Revolver capacity binding.

-   Access to equity markets in time frame assessed.

-   Supplier recovery probability not overstated.

-   Board\'s tolerance for dilution vs insolvency risk understood.

**Stage 5 --- Risk and Control (Where Are We Exposed?)**

Stress test concentration and reserve risks. Surviving base case doesn\'t mean company is strong. Combined stress scenario reveals structural weakness.

**Output: Stress Test Analysis Sheet**

-   Individual stress cases with positive surpluses.

-   Combined stress (recall +20%, insurance denial, revenue -15%): deficit \$31.5M.

-   Supplier section: Supplier A at 62% --- marked CRITICAL.

-   Customer details: Customer X 28%, top 3 customers 48%.

-   Reserves: liquidity coverage 2.73x, reserves at 13% of gross costs, 15.2 months of operating cash flow coverage.

-   \$31.5M deficit clearly highlighted.

**Verify**

-   Combined stress scenario realistic, not arbitrary.

-   Supplier concentration 62% single source --- operationally confirmed.

-   Customer concentration figures including large single OEM exposure.

-   Insurance limits legally enforceable.

-   Reserve levels align with internal policy.

-   Liquidity buffer policy defined.

-   Combined stress funding gap → capital contingency plan in place.

**End State**

Single structural risk view with three findings:

4.  Individually survivable shocks.

5.  Combined stress funding gap of \$31.5M.

6.  High supplier concentration as structural vulnerability requiring mitigation.
